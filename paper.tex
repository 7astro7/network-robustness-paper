\begin{abstract}
Power-law networks tolerate extensive random failures yet can collapse abruptly under targeted
attacks, often with little warning from connectivity measures alone. We ask whether an
information-theoretic rate of structural change can provide early warning of impending
connectivity collapse in such networks, and under what conditions any such signal transfers
across generative ensembles.

We track EWMA-smoothed successive Kullback--Leibler divergence between adjacent empirical
degree distributions in Chung--Lu scale-free graphs with $2<\gamma<3$ under both random and
targeted node removal. Using fixed, pre-specified detection rules that do not reference
connectivity, we find that the signal departs from an initial low-activity baseline well
before GCC collapse under random failure, a pattern confirmed on an empirical CAIDA
AS-level Internet topology. Under targeted removal, the signal exhibits immediate disruption
rather than advance warning. A degree-matched configuration-model experiment reveals that
the same rule fails due to ensemble-induced threshold inflation, a mechanistically
interpretable limitation on transferability that motivates ensemble-aware deployment of
structural monitoring rules.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{power-law networks, network robustness, KL divergence, early warning signals, percolation, complex networks, information theory, degree distribution}

\section*{Introduction and Motivation}
Many real-world communication, social, and biological networks are known to exhibit
heavy-tailed degree distributions~\cite{newman2003structure,barabasi1999scaling}.

For graphs $G=(V,E)$ with degree counts $\{k_i\}$, one often observes
\[
P(k) \propto k^{-\gamma}, \qquad \gamma \in (2,3),
\]
reflecting extreme heterogeneity and the presence of high-degree hubs. Network robustness
is traditionally evaluated using the giant connected component (GCC) fraction $S(q)$ under
random or targeted node removal. However, this connectivity-based perspective provides
limited insight into how topological structure degrades prior to global fragmentation. As
emphasized in recent reviews, robustness transitions in complex networks often occur
abruptly and may exhibit little to no reliable early-warning signals when assessed using
classical
indicators such as the GCC \cite{radicchi2024robustness}. This lack of anticipatory
indicators poses significant challenges for monitoring the stability of real-world
networks. A complementary line of work studies early-warning signals in dynamical
processes on networks, where instability is approached through changes in node-state
statistics rather than through structural damage. For example, Suweis and
D'Odorico~\cite{suweis2014earlywarning}
analyze multivariate stochastic dynamics on interaction networks and show that rising
variance and cross-correlation can anticipate loss of stability in the underlying
networked system. Our setting differs in that we consider progressive structural
degradation under node removal, where no node-state time series is assumed; we therefore
seek early warning directly from topological reorganization, using successive KL
divergence between degree distributions as a purely structural signal.
This work is conceptually related to the broader early-warning literature on critical
transitions~\cite{scheffer2009earlywarning,dakos2012methods}, but differs in that we
observe only structural change rather than node-state dynamics.
Our results are correspondingly structured: a positive controlled finding in the Chung--Lu
ensemble, confirmed by an out-of-sample empirical validation on an Internet topology,
together with a mechanistically interpretable failure of transfer to the configuration-model
ensemble under identical detection settings, a finding that motivates ensemble-aware
deployment of structural monitoring rules.

Information-theoretic approaches offer a complementary perspective to connectivity-based
robustness measures by quantifying distributional change rather than component-level size.
In particular, the Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence between degree distributions at
successive damage levels captures rates of structural reorganization and may reveal early
signs of impending network collapse. The central questions of this work are:

\begin{quote}
Can information-theoretic rates of structural change in degree distribution provide
early-warning signals of impending connectivity collapse in power-law networks under progressive
node removal? And under what conditions does such a signal transfer across generative
ensembles with matched tail exponents?
\end{quote}

We answer the first question affirmatively within the Chung--Lu ensemble and on empirical
data, and show for the second that transferability is ensemble-dependent in a mechanistically
interpretable way.

Although information-theoretic quantities have been used to characterize static graph
structure, their behavior under
progressive damage remains largely unexplored. Dehmer~(2008)~\cite{dehmer2008information}
introduced Shannon-entropy measures to quantify graph complexity, and subsequent work has
examined entropy-based summaries of degree heterogeneity or structural diversity. However,
these studies focus on fixed network topologies and do not connect entropy to dynamical
processes such as node failures, percolation transitions, or GCC collapse. Likewise, the
extensive literature on network robustness (ranging from classical results on error and
attack tolerance
in scale-free graphs \cite{albert2000error,cohen2000resilience} to modern treatments of
resilience
\cite{radicchi2024robustness}) relies primarily on quantities such as degree moments,
spectral radius, or percolation thresholds. Very little attention is given to
information-theoretic indicators that might change before observable topological collapse.

Unlike entropy, which summarizes a distribution at a fixed damage level, KL divergence
enables comparison of successive states and is therefore sensitive to rates of structural
change. The KL divergence
$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q)$ quantifies
the information-theoretic distance between two probability distributions, measuring how
much additional information is required to encode samples from $P$ using a code optimized
for $Q$. When applied to successive degree distributions
$P_{q+\Delta q}$ and $P_q$, this quantity provides a per-step proxy for structural reorganization over removal
increments $\Delta q$ as damage accumulates. Unlike moment-based statistics such as mean
degree or degree variance, KL divergence is sensitive to the full shape of the
distribution and can detect subtle shifts in connectivity patterns before they manifest as
macroscopic changes. The asymmetric nature of KL divergence provides a temporal signal
that naturally captures directional changes in structural degradation.

To our knowledge, no prior work has systematically tracked the KL divergence of the degree
distribution as a function of node-removal probability, nor evaluated whether KL
divergence exhibits predictive signatures prior to critical
fragmentation points. This gap motivates our investigation of $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ as a
potential early-warning signal of structural failure in power-law networks.

\paragraph{Scope.}
We use EWMA-smoothed successive KL divergence between adjacent degree distributions to detect the onset of nonstationary structural reorganization under progressive node removal, using fixed decision rules that do not reference connectivity.
We do not estimate the percolation threshold $q_c$; in the targeted regime, the same signal is interpreted as immediate disruption with an intensity-based summary rather than advance warning of collapse.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Rate-based structural signal.} We track EWMA-smoothed successive KL divergence between adjacent empirical degree distributions as a per-step change-rate along the damage grid.
    \item \textbf{Random-failure warning via baseline departure.} Using a fixed baseline window and threshold rule that does not reference connectivity, the signal departs from an initial stationary regime at $q_{\mathrm{warn}} \ll q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$ across a $\gamma$ sweep in Chung--Lu graphs.
    \item \textbf{Targeted regime: intensity-only summary.} Under hub-first removal the signal exhibits immediate disruption at the grid floor; we summarize targeted disruption by the initial shock magnitude $I_{\mathrm{tgt}}:=\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{1/2})$ together with the collapse proxy $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$.
    \item \textbf{Validation and ensemble experiment.} We reproduce the random-failure pattern on a CAIDA AS graph and show, via a degree-matched configuration-model experiment, that the rule fails in 292/360 runs due to structural-churn-induced threshold inflation during the baseline window, a mechanistically interpretable failure mode.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Problem Definition}

Let $G_0$ denote an undamaged network with degree distribution $P_0(k)$. Consider
progressive node removal at fraction $q$ (random or targeted), producing a damaged graph
$G_q$ with observed distribution $P_q(k)$. We define the successive Kullback--Leibler divergence
between degree distributions at adjacent damage levels:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_{q+\Delta q} \| P_q) = \sum_k P_{q+\Delta q}(k) \log_2 \frac{P_{q+\Delta
q}(k)}{P_q(k)}.
\]

We use $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ as a per-step proxy for structural reorganization per removal
increment $\Delta q$, and evaluate
how its behavior relates to:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item the GCC fraction $S(q)$ and the collapse proxy $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$ (defined
    by $S(q)<0.1$),
    \item warning timing $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ under random removal,
    \item warning lead time $\Delta_{\mathrm{warn}}=q_{\mathrm{collapse}}-q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ under random removal,
    \item robustness of these statistics across the $\gamma$ sweep.
\end{itemize}

Our goal is not to modify the network locally, but to evaluate how global,
information-theoretic measures of distributional change reflect the onset of
fragmentation.

\subsection*{Theoretical Background}

For uncorrelated random graphs, let $k$ denote the degree of a randomly chosen node, with
$\langle k \rangle$ and $\langle k^2 \rangle$ denoting the first and second moments of the
degree distribution. The Molloy--Reed criterion~\cite{molloy1995critical,molloy1998size}
states that a giant connected component exists when
\[
\kappa = \frac{\langle k^2 \rangle - \langle k \rangle}{\langle k \rangle} > 1.
\]
Under independent random node failures with probability $q$, this condition yields an
approximate
critical threshold
\[
(1 - q)\kappa \approx 1
\qquad \Rightarrow \qquad
q_c \approx 1 - \frac{\langle k \rangle}{\langle k^2 \rangle - \langle k \rangle}.
\]

This framework highlights that classical percolation theory characterizes collapse in
terms of low-order moments of the degree distribution~\cite{dorogovtsev2008critical}.
However, many distinct degree
distributions can share the same values of $\langle k \rangle$ and $\langle k^2 \rangle$,
particularly in heterogeneous networks. As a result, substantial redistribution of
connectivity can occur while moment-based quantities change only gradually.

Progressive node removal induces continuous reshaping of the degree distribution well
before the percolation threshold is reached. Under random failure, high-degree nodes are
removed infrequently at early stages, leading to slow erosion of the tail, whereas
targeted removal produces immediate and asymmetric distortion of the distribution. These
structural changes can accumulate while the GCC fraction $S(q)$ remains largely unchanged,
so they may be poorly reflected by connectivity-based summaries until near fragmentation.

Although KL divergence does not appear explicitly in the Molloy--Reed condition, it is
sensitive to such redistribution because it compares the full degree distributions at
successive damage levels. Our objective is therefore not to predict the collapse point
itself, but to detect the onset of structural reorganization that ultimately leads to
abrupt fragmentation, an issue emphasized in recent reviews \cite{radicchi2024robustness}.

\section*{Hypotheses}

Classical analyses of network robustness emphasize connectivity-based quantities such as
the size of the giant connected component and the location of percolation thresholds.
While these measures characterize the point of global failure, they provide limited
information about how structural degradation unfolds prior to collapse. In this work, we
instead focus on the rate of structural change in the network's degree distribution as
damage accumulates.

We formulate the following hypotheses regarding the behavior of information-theoretic
change rates under different node-removal regimes, along with explicit empirical
predictions.

\subsection*{H1: Stationarity under Random Failure}

Under random node removal, Chung--Lu 
power-law graphs exhibit an initial regime in which structural changes are small 
and approximately stationary. We hypothesize that, at low
damage levels, the successive KL change-rate signal fluctuates weakly around a stable
baseline, reflecting the network’s tolerance to random failure.

As damage accumulates, this stationarity is eventually lost prior to macroscopic
fragmentation.

\paragraph{Predictions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The successive KL divergence remains in a comparatively low-activity regime for small values
    of $q$, enabling baseline calibration over a fixed low-damage window.
    \item A detectable deviation from this baseline occurs at a damage
    level $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ strictly less than the collapse point
    $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$.
    \item The warning point $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ exhibits moderate variability across
    repeated runs with different random seeds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{H2: Immediate Nonstationarity under Targeted Failure}

Under targeted (hub-first) node removal, structural degradation begins immediately.
Because high-degree nodes are removed first, the degree distribution is altered
substantially even at small damage fractions. We hypothesize that, in this regime, the
successive KL change-rate signal does not admit a low-damage calibration window.

Instead, the successive KL change-rate signal is immediately nonstationary, with large
early deviations that may not evolve monotonically. The following predictions apply to 
targeted (hub-first) removal.

\paragraph{Predictions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The successive KL divergence does not admit a low-damage calibration window at small
    values of $q$.
    \item The signal shows a pronounced early deviation caused by immediate removal of
    high-degree nodes.
    \item The earliest reportable targeted response occurs at the grid floor midpoint
    $q_{1/2}=\Delta q/2$; we summarize targeted disruption by the initial shock magnitude
    $I_{\mathrm{tgt}}:=\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{1/2})$ together with the collapse proxy
    $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$.
\end{itemize}


\subsection*{H3: Robustness across the Power-Law Regime}

The qualitative behaviors described in H1 and H2 are hypothesized to persist across
the Chung--Lu ensemble for degree exponents in the range
\[
2 < \gamma < 3.
\]

\paragraph{Predictions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Under random failure and for all values of $\gamma$ in this range, an
    early-warning point
    $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ can be identified using the same successive KL signal.
    \item Under random failure, the relative ordering $q_{\mathrm{warn}} <
    q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$ holds
    consistently across $\gamma$.
    \item Under random failure, while the mean value of $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ may vary with
    $\gamma$, the
    existence of an early-warning regime is robust to changes in degree heterogeneity.
\end{itemize}


\section*{Metrics}

To evaluate the hypotheses outlined previously we require a metric that captures how
rapidly network structure changes as node removal progresses, rather than the absolute
level of connectivity or heterogeneity at a fixed damage level. All metrics in this work
are derived from the evolution of the degree distribution under progressive node removal.

\subsection*{Degree Distribution as Structural State}

At each damage fraction $q$, we compute the empirical degree distribution
\[
P_q(k) = \frac{1}{|V(G_q)|} \sum_{v \in V(G_q)} \mathbf{1}\{\deg(v) = k\},
\]
where $G_q$ denotes the graph after a fraction $q$ of nodes has been removed. The degree
distribution serves as a compact marginal summary of network structure,
capturing how node connectivity levels redistribute as damage accumulates.

\subsection*{Successive Kullback--Leibler Divergence}

To quantify incremental structural change, we compute the successive Kullback--Leibler
(KL) divergence between degree distributions at adjacent damage levels:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(P_{q+\Delta q} \,\|\, P_q\right)
=
\sum_k
P_{q+\Delta q}(k)
\log_2
\frac{P_{q+\Delta q}(k)}{P_q(k)}, 
\]
where $\Delta q$ denotes the removal increment between successive damage levels.

Unlike KL divergence relative to a fixed reference distribution, the successive form
measures the per-step rate at which the network's degree structure changes along the damage grid. Large values indicate abrupt reorganization of connectivity patterns, while
small values correspond to gradual or stationary structural evolution. Because $q$ is evaluated on a finite grid and the signal is EWMA-smoothed, we interpret successive KL as a per-step change-rate along the damage grid rather than a continuous-time derivative. For reference, we
also define the degree entropy at damage level $q$ as
\[
H(q) = -\sum_k P_q(k)\log_2 P_q(k),
\]
which summarizes degree heterogeneity at a fixed structural state.

While degree entropy $H(q)$ summarizes heterogeneity of the degree distribution at a fixed
damage level, successive Kullback--Leibler divergence explicitly encodes change between
successive states of damage. Consequently, KL divergence can be sensitive to structural
reorganization even in regimes where entropy evolves gradually, motivating use of entropy as a
complementary indicator for early warning.

\paragraph{Secondary control signals.}
In addition to successive KL, we evaluate two comparison signals in the Controls section:
(i) the successive Jensen--Shannon (JS) divergence~\cite{lin1991js} between adjacent
degree distributions,
\[
D_{\mathrm{JS}}(P_{q+\Delta q},P_q)=\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_{q+\Delta q}\|M)+\tfrac12
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_q\|M),
\qquad
M=\tfrac12(P_{q+\Delta q}+P_q),
\]
and (ii) absolute entropy change,
\[
|\Delta H(q)| = \big|H(q+\Delta q)-H(q)\big|.
\]
These are used only to assess specificity of the baseline-deviation rule and are not the
primary signal of interest.

We leave ordering-aware distances (e.g., Wasserstein on degree) for future comparator
extensions.

\paragraph{Moment-based comparator (Molloy--Reed branching factor).}
Because classical percolation thresholds depend on low-order degree moments, we also
compute the Molloy--Reed branching factor
\[
\kappa(q)=\frac{\langle k^2\rangle_q-\langle k\rangle_q}{\langle k\rangle_q},
\]
with moments taken from the empirical $P_q(k)$. We smooth $\kappa(q)$ with the same EWMA
used for $\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q)$ and apply the same baseline-deviation rule as a
simple benchmark. Comparator results are reported in the Supplement.

\paragraph{Support and numerical stability.}
Because $P_q(k)$ is estimated empirically from a finite graph, some degree values may
attain zero observed probability
at intermediate damage levels. To ensure $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_{q+\Delta q}\,\|\,P_q)$ is
well-defined across all $q$, we evaluate degree distributions on a fixed discrete support
for each run and apply a small additive regularization
$P_q(k)\leftarrow P_q(k)+\varepsilon$ before normalization, with $\varepsilon = 10^{-12}$.

\subsection*{Signal Smoothing}

The raw successive KL divergence may exhibit high-frequency fluctuations arising from
finite-size effects and stochastic variability in node removal. To suppress such noise
while preserving trend information, we apply an exponentially weighted moving average
(EWMA) to the successive KL sequence:
\[
\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i)
=
\alpha D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i)
+
(1-\alpha)\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{i-1}).
\]

The smoothing parameter is fixed at $\alpha=0.2$ across all experiments as a pre-specified
value; no post-hoc tuning is performed. Sensitivity of warning timing to $\alpha$ and
threshold $z$ is reported in Appendix~\nameref{sec:appendix}.
The EWMA assigns exponentially decaying weight to past observations, corresponding to an
effective memory on the order of $1/\alpha$.

The smoothed signal $\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q)$ is the sole warning observable
used throughout this study; alternative signals are evaluated only in control comparisons.


\subsection*{Early-Warning Decision Rules}
Decision rules for interpreting the smoothed successive KL signal are specified in
Sec.~\nameref{sec:early_warning_criteria}. We define distinct criteria for random and
targeted removal while using the same underlying signal.

\section*{Experimental Design}

Our experimental design isolates how information-theoretic change rates behave under
progressive network damage across different structural regimes. We vary (i) the degree
heterogeneity of the underlying network and (ii) the node-removal mechanism, while holding
all detection logic fixed. For each configuration, we measure the separation between an
information-theoretic warning point and the onset of macroscopic connectivity
collapse.

\subsection*{Graph Model}

The primary synthetic networks in this work are generated using the Chung--Lu expected-degree model
~\cite{chunglu2002connected}
with power-law degree sequences. In addition, we analyze an empirical CAIDA AS graph and run a degree-matched configuration-model experiment to assess ensemble transfer. This choice isolates the role of degree heterogeneity
while avoiding degree--degree correlations and growth effects inherent to other generative
mechanisms.


\paragraph{Configuration-model ensemble experiment.}
To probe sensitivity to the generating ensemble, we repeat the random-failure pipeline using
a configuration-model graph class matched by tail exponent $\gamma$.
Construction details are deferred to the Appendix; findings are summarized in the Results
configuration-model check subsection.

\subsection*{Failure Regimes}

We consider two node-removal regimes: (i) random failure, in which nodes are removed
uniformly at random, and (ii) targeted failure, in which nodes are removed in descending
order of degree (hub-first removal). In both cases, node removal proceeds incrementally as
a function of the removal fraction $q$, and all observables are evaluated on the resulting
damaged graph $G_q$.


\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Network Model}
We study robustness in Chung--Lu power-law graphs with degree distributions
\[
P(k) \propto k^{-\gamma},
\qquad 2 < \gamma < 3,
\]
a regime characteristic of many empirical infrastructure and communication networks.
Unless otherwise stated, all networks contain $N = 10{,}000$ nodes.
This size is held fixed across all synthetic experiments to isolate the effect
of degree heterogeneity; the dependence of results on $N$ is discussed in the Conclusion.
Unlike the configuration model, which can exhibit structural degree correlations in
heavy-tailed networks as a finite-size artifact, Chung--Lu assigns edges independently.
This motivates use of Chung--Lu for the main controlled analysis.

To examine sensitivity to degree heterogeneity, we sweep
\[
\gamma \in \{2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, 2.6, 2.7, 2.8, 2.9\}.
\]
For each value of $\gamma$, results are averaged across multiple random seeds to account
for stochastic variability in network realization and node removal.

\subsection*{Failure Models}
The two failure regimes introduced in the Experimental Design section are implemented as
follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Random failure: nodes are removed uniformly at random.
    \item Targeted failure: nodes are removed in descending order of degree (hub-first
    removal).
\end{enumerate}

For each failure mode, a fraction $q \in [0, 0.9]$ of nodes is progressively removed.
All structural observables are computed on the resulting damaged graph $G_q$.
Simulations and graph computations are implemented using NetworkX~\cite{SciPyProceedings_11}.

\subsection*{Structural Observables}
At each damage level $q$, we compute:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The giant connected component fraction
    \[
    S(q) = \frac{| \mathrm{GCC}(G_q) |}{|V(G_q)|},
    \]
    used only to contextualize collapse.
    \item The empirical degree distribution $P_q(k)$ of $G_q$.
\end{itemize}

No connectivity-based quantity is used in the detection logic.

\subsection*{Successive KL Divergence}
The successive KL divergence signal, its numerical regularization, and EWMA smoothing are
defined in the Metrics section. Here we use the resulting smoothed sequence
$\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q)$ as the sole structural change-rate observable.

\subsection*{Early-Warning Criteria}
\phantomsection
\label{sec:early_warning_criteria}
Rather than introducing multiple metrics, we apply regime-appropriate decision rules to
the same successive KL signal.

\subsubsection*{Random Failure: Baseline Deviation Rule}
Under random failure, networks exhibit an initial low-activity regime in which
$\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q)$ fluctuates weakly around a constant baseline.
We define a baseline window
\[
q \le 0.15,
\]
and compute the baseline mean $\mu_0$ and standard deviation $\sigma_0$ over this range.
We choose $q\le 0.15$ as a fixed low-damage calibration window.
This window is wide enough to accumulate sufficient observations for a stable noise-floor estimate
$(\mu_0,\sigma_0)$ (the baseline contains approximately 15 midpoints on our 100-step grid) while
remaining narrow enough to precede the sustained departures observed empirically across all $\gamma$
values in the sweep.
Across random-failure runs, this early region precedes the sustained departures used for detection,
and provides a consistent noise-floor estimate $(\mu_0,\sigma_0)$ for the baseline-deviation rule.
The window is held constant across all $\gamma$ and is not tuned post hoc.
Sensitivity of warning timing to EWMA smoothing $\alpha$ and threshold $z$ in the rule
$\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}} > \mu_0 + z\sigma_0$ is reported in
\autoref{tab:sensitivity_alpha_z}.

The early-warning point $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ is defined as the smallest $q$ such that
\[
\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q) > \mu_0 + 2\sigma_0.
\]

If no $q$ exceeds the threshold over the sweep, we record
$q_{\mathrm{warn}}=\mathrm{NaN}$ for that run and report detection rates $[n_{\mathrm{det}}/n]$.
Summary statistics (median [IQR]) are computed only over seeds where the quantity is
defined.

This criterion detects the loss of structural stationarity without reference to collapse
thresholds or numerical derivatives.

\subsubsection*{Targeted Failure: Intensity Summary (no timing estimand)}
Under targeted (hub-first) removal, structural disruption begins immediately and the
successive-KL signal does not admit a low-damage calibration window. Because successive
KL is evaluated on midpoint support, the earliest reportable value occurs at
$q_{1/2}=\Delta q/2$; in practice the targeted response triggers at this grid floor, so
we do not treat timing as a meaningful estimand.
Instead, we summarize targeted disruption by the initial shock magnitude
\[
I_{\mathrm{tgt}} := \tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{1/2}),
\]
reported across seeds together with the collapse proxy $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$ (defined
by $S(q)<0.1$). This retains a connectivity-free targeted summary while avoiding
overinterpretation of timing under hub-first removal.

\subsection*{Collapse Identification}
For post hoc comparison only (not used in any detection rule), we define the collapse
proxy $q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$ as the smallest $q$ such that
\[
S(q) < 0.1.
\]
This threshold is not used in the detection logic and serves solely to contextualize
warning lead times.
As a result, changing this proxy threshold affects only the reported lead times, not the
detected warning points.


\subsection*{Experimental Protocol}
For synthetic experiments under random failure, we use a uniform damage grid $q_0,q_1,\dots,q_{99}$ spanning
$[0,0.9]$, with increment $\Delta q = 0.9/99$ (i.e., 100 evenly spaced values). For targeted removal, we use a higher-resolution
grid of 400 evenly spaced values over $[0,0.9]$.
Each configuration is repeated across 40 random seeds.

For each value of $\gamma$, we summarize random-failure warning timing and lead time, and report targeted collapse timing together with the initial disruption intensity $I_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ across seeds.
CAIDA experiments use a separate damage grid (e.g., $q_{\max}=0.99$ with 200 points) as
specified in the CAIDA figure pipeline.

\subsection*{Empirical network: CAIDA AS graph (\CAIDASnapshot)}
We use the CAIDA AS Relationships Dataset (serial-2; snapshot
\CAIDASnapshot)~\cite{caida_as_rel_20260101}. We parse
\texttt{AS1|AS2|rel|source}, drop comment lines, and form an undirected simple graph from
unique AS pairs.

\subsection*{Datasets analyzed and where results appear}
This study reports results on three network classes:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Chung--Lu networks (primary controlled analysis):}
    Chung--Lu expected-degree graphs with power-law degree sequences, swept over
    $\gamma \in (2,3)$.
    We evaluate both random failure and targeted (hub-first) removal in this ensemble,
    using fixed detection logic and reporting $q_{\mathrm{warn}}$ (random) and $I_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ (targeted).

    \item \textbf{Empirical network (out-of-sample validation):}
    the CAIDA AS Relationships graph (snapshot \CAIDASnapshot).
    This serves as a realism check of the random-failure baseline-departure pattern
    observed in the synthetic setting.

    \item \textbf{Configuration-model networks (ensemble experiment):}
    a degree-matched configuration-model graph class used only under random failure
    to probe ensemble sensitivity when the tail exponent is matched but the generating mechanism differs.
\end{itemize}

Unless explicitly labeled as CAIDA or configuration-model, all figures and tables in the
Results section refer to the synthetic Chung--Lu experiments.

\section*{Analysis and Results}

We present results in the order: (i) a representative synthetic Chung--Lu run under
random failure to illustrate the signal relative to $S(q)$, (ii) an empirical CAIDA AS graph
random-failure case study as an out-of-sample validation, (iii) a representative synthetic
targeted (hub-first) Chung--Lu run to examine how degree-targeted removal accelerates collapse relative to random failure, (iv) $\gamma$-sweep
summaries for Chung--Lu via a plot and tables across seeds, and (v) a configuration-model
random-failure experiment to assess ensemble transfer under matched $\gamma$.
We then report two controls (no-damage and damage-without-collapse) to evaluate false-trigger
behavior under the same baseline-deviation rule.

\subsection*{Ensemble sensitivity: configuration-model check}
\label{sec:config_model_check_results}
To assess transfer beyond the expected-degree Chung--Lu ensemble, we repeat the random-failure
$\gamma$ sweep using a degree-matched configuration-model network class (degree-sequence constrained;
see Appendix \nameref{sec:config_model_check} for construction details). All failure, smoothing, baseline,
and decision-rule settings are held fixed.

Under identical settings, detections are substantially sparser in the configuration model than in Chung--Lu
(68/360 runs versus 357/360), summarized in \autoref{tab:config_random} below. This stark asymmetry
constitutes a finding in its own right: the successive-KL signal is sensitive to ensemble structure beyond the tail exponent, and matching $\gamma$ alone is insufficient to predict signal behavior when the generative mechanism differs.

\paragraph{Ensemble structure governs transferability.}
This result has a natural mechanistic interpretation. Successive KL is sensitive not only to the
tail exponent but to how degree mass redistributes under removal in the underlying ensemble.
In Chung--Lu, degrees are realized as fluctuations around expected values, so progressive random
removal induces smooth, distributed drift in the empirical degree histogram that accumulates into
a sustained baseline departure. In the configuration model, the hard degree-sequence constraint imposed at construction
produces immediate structural churn even during the low-damage baseline window $q\le 0.15$:
the KL signal is elevated from the outset, inflating the estimated noise floor $(\mu_0,\sigma_0)$
and raising the detection threshold $\mu_0+2\sigma_0$ above the level the signal subsequently
reaches. The failure to detect is therefore not due to signal absence but due to an elevated
noise floor caused by the ensemble structure itself.
\autoref{tab:baseline_noise_comparison} quantifies the noise-floor difference: mean $\sigma_0$ in the baseline window is substantially elevated in the configuration model relative to Chung--Lu at every $\gamma$, consistent with structural churn inflating the estimated noise floor. In 276 of the 292 failed configuration-model runs, the resulting threshold $\mu_0+2\sigma_0$ exceeded the maximum post-baseline signal value, confirming that detection failure is predominantly attributable to threshold inflation rather than signal absence.

\input{paper/tables/baseline_noise_comparison.tex}

For network classes where ensemble properties are known a priori, as is often the case for well-studied 
infrastructure or communication topologies, a pre-calibrated threshold could replace the data-driven baseline, 
restoring signal transferability without requiring per-ensemble recalibration. Characterizing which structural
properties beyond the one-point degree distribution govern ensemble-stable transfer is a
well-posed direction for follow-up study.

\section*{Conclusion}

We asked whether a purely structural, information-theoretic change-rate signal can provide
advance notice of connectivity collapse in heavy-tailed networks under progressive
node removal. Across Chung--Lu power-law graphs with $2<\gamma<3$, we tracked the
smoothed successive KL divergence between adjacent empirical degree distributions and
applied fixed, pre-specified detection rules without using connectivity in the warning
logic. Our primary controlled experiments use the Chung--Lu expected-degree ensemble to isolate
distributional drift under progressive damage; an additional configuration-model ensemble experiment probes
transfer to a degree-sequence-constrained ensemble.

Under random failure, the successive KL signal displays an initial low-activity regime
followed by a sustained departure from baseline that consistently precedes GCC collapse,
yielding substantial warning lead time across the $\gamma$ sweep in the Chung--Lu ensemble.
The same qualitative pattern appears in the case study on the CAIDA AS graph, suggesting that
the signal is capturing genuine structural reorganization rather than an artifact of a particular
synthetic ensemble.

Under targeted (hub-first) removal, the interpretation changes: structural disruption is
immediate, and the successive-KL signal exhibits an initial shock at the grid floor
$q_{1/2}=\Delta q/2$. We summarize targeted disruption by the initial shock magnitude
$I_{\mathrm{tgt}}:=\tilde{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{1/2})$ together with the collapse proxy
$q_{\mathrm{collapse}}$, which remains abrupt and occurs at small $q$ under hub-first removal.
This accords with the finding that scale-free networks are highly vulnerable 
to intentional (hub-targeted) attacks, with rapid GCC disintegration after removing 
only a small fraction of the highest-degree 
nodes~\cite{albert2000error,cohen2001breakdown}.

Three controls clarify what the baseline-deviation rule is (and is not) detecting. A
no-damage null control produces no false triggers, while a damage-without-collapse control
shows that entropy-change-based signals can trigger frequently even when the GCC remains
far from collapse, whereas successive KL remains specific under the same rule.
A moment-based comparator, the Molloy--Reed branching factor $\kappa(q)$ smoothed and
evaluated under the identical EWMA and baseline-deviation rule, produces zero detections
across all 40 seeds at $\gamma=2.5$ (Appendix~\nameref{sec:kappa_benchmark}).
This null result directly answers the question of whether a simpler low-order-moment
summary could substitute for the full distributional signal: it cannot, under the same
decision rule. Taken together, these results support successive KL divergence as a
simple and interpretable rate-based indicator that complements connectivity-based
robustness analysis by highlighting the onset of structural change before macroscopic
fragmentation, in a way that low-order moment summaries do not.

\begin{samepage}
In this study we intentionally restrict the structural state to the empirical degree distribution to keep the signal interpretable and the decision rule fixed across regimes.
Richer summaries (e.g., joint-degree structure, mixing, motifs) may refine or alter warning behavior, especially under adversarial removal.
In addition, the configuration-model experiment demonstrates that transfer is ensemble-dependent under identical settings even when the tail exponent is matched: structural churn during the baseline window inflates the estimated noise floor, raising the detection threshold above the level the post-baseline signal reaches in 276 of the 292 non-detecting runs (out of 360 total). This finding goes beyond H3, which concerns robustness within the Chung--Lu ensemble across $\gamma$. H3 is supported by the results; the configuration-model experiment reveals a distinct limitation on transferability, motivating future work on adaptive calibration strategies that account for ensemble-specific noise floor behavior, such as pre-calibrating the detection threshold from ensemble structure rather than from a data-driven baseline window.
All synthetic experiments use $N=10{,}000$ nodes; we have not systematically varied network
size. Finite-size scaling theory predicts that the percolation transition sharpens with
increasing $N$~\cite{molloy1995critical}, which would produce a more abrupt GCC drop and
potentially reduce detection variance. The CAIDA AS graph ($N\approx 78{,}000$ nodes) provides
one cross-scale data point: the 40-seed detection rate is 40/40 and the qualitative signal
shape matches the synthetic results, offering limited but encouraging evidence that the
behavior is not confined to the $N=10{,}000$ regime. Systematic finite-size analysis remains
future work.
Natural next steps are to track change rates in higher-order structural features and to test analogous rules under link removal and correlated or temporally clustered failure processes.
\end{samepage}
